VR Training

The process of utilizing VR to teach various knowledge and skills to students, employees, or any other person interested in learning.

VR Training

Since this time, VR training programs have grown to fulfill a variety of use cases in a multitude of industries.

Immersion–Presence Theory

VR’s immersive qualities (HMDs, sensory engagement, vivid environments) increase presence, which enhances attention, engagement, and learning.

Immersion → Presence (moderate effect). Presence → higher engagement, motivation, and learning.

Slater & Wilbur (1997); Cummings & Bailenson (2016); Dubovi et al. (2017)

Task–Technology Fit

Training is most effective when VR technology closely matches the real-world tasks, tools, and motor actions required.

High task–technology fit shows significantly larger performance effects (d = .82) than moderate (d = .45) or low (d = .21).

Goodhue & Thompson (1995); Howard et al. (2021)

Novelty Effect

VR’s novelty increases motivation and engagement early on, potentially elevating performance effects—though these effects may diminish over time.

Meta-analytic evidence shows effect sizes shrinking across years, possibly due to reduced novelty or wider application contexts.

Freina & Ott (2015); Pan et al. (2006); Howard et al. (2021)

The Role of AI in VR Training

DeFabiis et al. (2025) found that only 2 of 23 studies examining AI in VR training contexts assess its effects on training outcomes

Truong et al. (2022) and Qi et al. (2021) found that AI feedback improved learning outcomes compared to no feedback in specific VR training scenarios

Hypothesis

Hypothesis 1: Trainees who receive AI-generated personalized feedback during a virtual reality training program will demonstrate higher learning outcomes than trainees who receive generic feedback, who in turn will demonstrate higher learning outcomes than trainees who receive no feedback.

Hypothesis 2: Trainees who receive AI-generated personalized feedback during a virtual reality training program will exhibit faster learning over time than trainees who receive generic feedback or no feedback.

Feedback: AI Condition

The feedback provided was task-focused and addressed the importance of scanning memberships (Task-Relevant: 5). While the user did not overtly seek participation from Amanda, they did engage her minimally by confirming understanding (Participative: 3). The feedback was generally clear, providing a specific action for improvement (scanning memberships) and explaining its importance (Specific: 4). The user encouraged reflection by discussing the broader implications of policy adherence, which is indicative of reflective feedback, though it could have been more in-depth (Reflective: 4). Overall, the feedback was effective, with some room for improvement in encouraging participative dialogue.

Feedback: AI Condition Example 2

The interaction is entirely inappropriate for a professional context. The feedback given does not focus on any job-related tasks or performance but revolves solely around personal characteristics and social interactions, making it ineffective (Task-Relevant Score: 1). There is no encouragement for participation in the feedback process as the user shuts down any attempt by the NPC to engage (Participative Score: 1). The feedback lacks specificity, clear strategies, or any pertinent work-related guidance (Specific Score: 1). There is a complete absence of encouragement for self-reflection on job performance (Reflective Score: 1). Overall, the conversation is not relevant to a professional setting or feedback process.

Measures: Cognitive Ability

ICAR16 (Condon & Revelle, 2014)

Contains four items each from four domains: verbal reasoning, letter and number series, three-dimensional rotation, and matrix reasoning

EFA demonstrated very good fit for a four-factor solution (Root Mean Squared Error of Approximation [RMSEA] = .014, Root Mean Square of the Residual [RMSR] = .01), Tucker Lewis Index [TLI] = .99).

Cronbach’s alpha of .81 and an omega total of .83

Concurrent and Construct Validity

The ICAR16 was found to correlate .59 with combined SAT scores and .52 with the ACT composite score

After correcting for both restricted range and reliability, the ICAR Sample Test showed correlations of .82 and .81 with the two respective composites (A and B) of the commercial cognitive ability measure, the Shipley-2.

The ICAR items demonstrated the ability to differentiate based on university majors; the rank ordering of mean ICAR scores by major correlated .75 with published rank orderings of mean combined SAT scores by major, and .86 with mean combined GRE scores by major.

Conscientiousness

Big Five Inventory–2 (BFI-2) Conscientiousness domain (Soto & John, 2017)

12 items; three 4-item facets: Organization, Productiveness, Responsibility.

5-point scale: 1 (Disagree strongly) to 5 (Agree strongly). Content-balanced (equal true-keyed/false-keyed items).

High internal consistency; α ≈ .86–.88 across validation samples. Hierarchical facet structure designed to increase descriptive precision and predictive power.

Novelty

Internal VR novelty scale (current study)

Multi-item internal scale. If internal consistency is inadequate, a single binary item will be used: “Are you relatively new to VR?” (yes/no).

Primary: Likert-type items (see Appendix E); internal consistency evaluated post-training. Fallback: binary yes/no item.

Internal consistency (e.g., Cronbach’s α) will be estimated post-training. If reliability is inadequate, analyses will use the binary novelty item instead of the scale.

Presence

iGroup Presence Questionnaire (IPQ; Schubert et al., 2001)

13 items; three components: Spatial Presence (5), Involvement (4), Realness (3), plus one general presence item.

5-point scales with anchors varying by item type (e.g., degree of presence, involvement, realness).

Three-component structure supported by EFA/CFA with good fit indices (e.g., RMSEA ≈ .02–.04). Factor loadings ~ .46–.88; KMO ≥ .83–.92. Replicated factor structure across heterogeneous samples and media types.

Self-Efficacy

Internal self-efficacy for feedback and goal-setting scales (Bandura et al., 2006 guidelines)

Two domain-specific scales: Feedback self-efficacy (4 items: task-relevant, participative, specific, reflective feedback) and goal-setting self-efficacy (3 items: specific/measurable goals, appropriate difficulty, participative goals).

11-point scale: 0 (Not confident at all) to 10 (Very confident). Rated immediately post-training for each domain.

Items aligned with trained performance dimensions and corresponding BARS. Scales designed following Bandura’s guidelines for domain-specific self-efficacy; internal consistency will be assessed prior to substantive analyses.

Simulation Sickness

Simulator Sickness Questionnaire (SSQ; Kennedy et al., 1993)

16 items; three subscales: Nausea, Oculomotor, Disorientation (some items load on multiple subscales); Total Severity score computed from all subscales.

4-point scale: 0 (None), 1 (Slight), 2 (Moderate), 3 (Severe), rated based on post-training experience.

Three-factor structure supported by factor analysis with acceptable eigenvalues and variance explained; subscales scaled to SD = 15 in calibration sample (~1,100 observations). Demonstrated utility in distinguishing high- vs. low-symptom simulators.

Utility Reactions

Internal Utility Reactions scale (current study; based on Kirkpatrick & Kirkpatrick, 1994; Alliger et al., 1997)

3 items: perceived preparation for real-world situations, perceived usefulness of skills/information, and intentions to use skills in the future.

5-point scale: 1 (Strongly disagree) to 5 (Strongly agree).

Constructed to assess a specific Level 1 criterion facet (utility). Prior meta-analytic work indicates utility reactions relate more strongly to learning and transfer than affective reactions such as enjoyment.

Other Personality Variables

BFI-2 Openness, Extraversion, Agreeableness, Neuroticism (Soto & John, 2017)

Four 12-item domain scales for Openness, Extraversion, Agreeableness, Neuroticism (standard BFI-2 domain scales).

5-point scale: 1 (Disagree strongly) to 5 (Agree strongly).

BFI-2 domains exhibit strong internal consistency and well-validated factor structure in large, diverse samples. Used as control/exploratory personality covariates.

Note. BFI-2 = Big Five Inventory–2; IPQ = iGroup Presence Questionnaire; SSQ = Simulator Sickness Questionnaire. Internal scales (novelty, self-efficacy, utility) will have internal consistency evaluated prior to inclusion in primary analyses.

Assumptions Testing

Summary of Multilevel Model Assumptions, Diagnostics, and Remedies

Assumption

What is assessed

Diagnostics

Indicators of violation

Potential remedies

Normality

Distribution of Level 1 residuals and empirical Bayes estimates of random effects (random intercepts and, if included, random slopes)

Q–Q plots and histograms; skewness and kurtosis statistics

|skewness| > 2 or |kurtosis| > 2 and clear visual departures from normality

Rely on robustness of MLM; if severe, consider robust standard errors or alternative distributional families (e.g., GLMM)

Homoscedasticity

Stability of Level 1 residual variance across fitted values and time (trial number)

Plots of standardized residuals versus fitted values and versus time

Funnel shapes or systematic increases/decreases in residual spread across fitted values or time

Model residual variance as a function of time and/or use heteroscedasticity-consistent (robust) standard errors

Linearity

Functional form of the relationship between predictors (e.g., time) and the outcome at Level 1

Plots of standardized residuals versus fitted values and versus time

Curvature or other non-random patterns in residuals suggesting mis-specified functional form

Add nonlinear terms (e.g., quadratic time) or theoretically justified interactions

Autocorrelation

Temporal dependence of Level 1 residuals across consecutively ordered trials within participants

Autocorrelation function (ACF) plots of Level 1 residuals

Clearly non-zero lag-1 autocorrelation (and potentially higher lags) in ACF plots

Add a first-order autoregressive [AR(1)] error structure and retain it only if it improves model fit (LRT, AIC, BIC)

Note. Assumptions will be evaluated using residual- and random-effect–based diagnostics. When violations are detected, model-based and/or robust adjustments will be considered and transparently reported.
